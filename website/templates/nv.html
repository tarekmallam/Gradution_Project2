Overview

Melanocytic Nevi (NV), commonly known as moles, are benign skin lesions formed by clusters of melanocytes. While most nevi are harmless and remain stable over time, monitoring is important as changes in their appearance may indicate potential risk.



Types of Melanocytic Nevi

  • Junctional Nevi: Typically flat, uniformly pigmented moles that occur at the junction of the epidermis and dermis.
  • Compound Nevi: Raised lesions with pigment distributed both in the epidermis and the dermis.
  • Intradermal Nevi: Usually flesh-colored, dome-shaped moles that are primarily located within the dermis.
  • Congenital Nevi: Present at birth and vary in size; larger congenital nevi may require closer observation.

Symptoms

  • Uniform color, often ranging from light brown to dark brown
  • Regular, well-defined borders
  • Generally small in size (typically less than 6mm in diameter)
  • Stable appearance over time unless changes occur

Causes



Melanocytic nevi develop due to a combination of genetic factors and sun exposure. While they are common in the general population, certain individuals may have a higher tendency to develop multiple moles.

Prevention

  • Limit excessive sun exposure and use broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) daily
  • Wear protective clothing, hats, and sunglasses when outdoors
  • Regularly examine your skin for any changes in moles or new lesions

Treatment Options



  • Observation: Most melanocytic nevi are monitored for any changes rather than removed immediately.
  • Surgical Excision: Recommended if a nevus shows signs of atypia or for cosmetic reasons.
  • Dermatoscopic Evaluation: Regular skin examinations by a dermatologist to assess any evolving features.

When to See a Doctor

Consult a dermatologist if you notice changes in the size, shape, or color of a mole, or if it becomes irregular, asymmetrical, or starts bleeding. Early detection of any malignant transformation is key.
