Overview



Actinic Keratosis (AK) is a rough, scaly patch on the skin caused by long-term exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation. Often considered a precancerous lesion, AK primarily appears on sun-exposed areas such as the face, ears, scalp, neck, and hands. Early detection and treatment are crucial to prevent potential progression to squamous cell carcinoma.

Types and Variants

  • Hyperkeratotic AK: Characterized by thick, scaly patches due to an overproduction of keratin.
  • Atrophic AK: Presents as thinner, less raised patches, often with a reddish hue.

Symptoms

  • Rough, dry, or scaly patches on the skin
  • Lesions that may be red, pink, or skin-colored
  • Mild itching or tenderness in the affected area
  • Persistent patches that do not heal

Causes



The primary cause of actinic keratosis is chronic exposure to UV radiation from the sun or tanning beds. Risk factors include fair skin, a history of sunburns, and prolonged outdoor activities without proper sun protection.

Prevention



  • Use broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) daily
  • Avoid sun exposure during peak hours (10 AM to 4 PM)
  • Wear protective clothing, hats, and sunglasses when outdoors
  • Regularly examine your skin for any changes or new lesions

Treatment Options

  • Cryotherapy: Freezing the lesion with liquid nitrogen is a common treatment.
  • Topical Medications: Creams such as 5-fluorouracil, imiquimod, or diclofenac can be used to treat AK.
  • Photodynamic Therapy: Involves applying a light-sensitive agent followed by exposure to a light source to destroy abnormal cells.
  • Curettage: Scraping away the lesion, sometimes combined with laser therapy.

When to See a Doctor

Consult a dermatologist if you notice any persistent or changing patches on your skin, especially on sun-exposed areas. Early evaluation and treatment are important to prevent progression to skin cancer.
